History of English Language:

The English language journey began with Old English around 450 AD, shaped by Germanic tribes. Middle English emerged after the Norman Conquest in 1066, blending French with Old English. Early Modern English (1500-1700) standardized due to the printing press and saw the works of Shakespeare enrich the language. Modern English (1700-present) spread globally via the British Empire, giving rise to various dialects. Throughout its evolution, English absorbed words from Latin, Greek, and other languages. Today, it's a global lingua franca, influenced by technology and globalization, with diverse forms like American, Australian, and Indian English, shaping communication worldwide.

Lesson 2: English Grammar:

Dear Learner, Now Let's dive into a basic English grammar lesson covering some essential concepts:

Parts of Speech:

  • Nouns
  • Pronouns
  • Verbs
  • Adjectives
  • Adverbs
  • Prepositions
  • Conjunctions
  • Interjections

Sentence Structure:

  • Simple sentences
  • Compound sentences
  • Complex sentences
  • Compound-complex sentences

Tenses:

  • Present simple
  • Present continuous
  • Present perfect
  • Past simple
  • Past continuous
  • Past perfect
  • Future simple

Modifiers:

  • Adjectives and adverbs are used as modifiers to provide more information about nouns (adjectives) or verbs/adjectives/adverbs (adverbs).

Subject-Verb Agreement:

  • Singular subjects and singular verbs
  • Plural subjects and plural verbs

Punctuation:

  • Periods
  • Commas
  • Semicolons
  • Colons
  • Question marks
  • Exclamation points
  • Quotation marks
  • Apostrophes

Word Order:

  • English generally follows a subject-verb-object (SVO) word order in declarative sentences.
  • In questions, the word order may change, with the verb typically preceding the subject.

Articles:

  • English has two articles: "a/an" (indefinite) and "the" (definite). Articles are used to specify or generalize nouns.

Concord:

  • Ensuring that words in a sentence agree in gender, number, and case is essential for grammatical correctness.

Clauses and Phrases:

  • Clauses are groups of words that contain a subject and a predicate.
  • Phrases are groups of words that function as a single unit but do not contain a subject and a predicate.
